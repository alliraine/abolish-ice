About the 287(g) Program

What is the 287(g) Program?

The 287(g) program is a controversial partnership between U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and local police departments. It allows ICE to delegate immigration enforcement powers to local law enforcement—turning sheriffs and deputies into de facto ICE agents. Civil rights groups argue that this blurs the line between community policing and immigration enforcement, fostering racial profiling and deepening fear in immigrant communities.

What are the 287(g) Models?

The 287(g) program operates through three models, all of which have raised serious concerns among immigrant advocates:

  • Jail Enforcement Model (JEM): Deputies screen people in jails for immigration status, often before they’ve had access to legal representation.
  • Warrant Service Officer (WSO): Deputies are authorized to serve ICE administrative warrants—civil, not criminal—on individuals in local custody.
  • Task Force Officer (TFO): Perhaps the most dangerous model, it gives officers ICE powers during regular policing in communities, leading to street-level immigration arrests without oversight.
How do law enforcement agencies join?

Agencies can opt into the program by submitting a letter of interest and signing a Memorandum of Agreement with ICE. This process lacks public input or oversight and can happen without community awareness. Once signed, officers are trained by ICE and begin enforcing immigration policy, often without transparency or accountability.
